Deliverable
60-second answer to "why don't real allocators just run Markowitz?"
Sample answer

Markowitz MVO is mathematically elegant but operationally broken. The problem is estimation error: optimizers take expected returns, variances, and covariances as inputs and maximize a function of those inputs. Since all three are estimated from noisy historical data, small changes in the inputs produce wildly different "optimal" portfolios. A 10 bps shift in expected return for one asset can swing its optimal weight from 0% to 40%.

Michaud called this "error maximization" — the assets with the highest estimated returns get overweighted, but those estimates are exactly where noise is highest. The output looks precise but isn't.

Real allocators fix this in three ways: (1) Black-Litterman starts from market-implied equilibrium and lets you tilt only where you have real views, with confidence weighting; (2) resampled efficient frontiers (Michaud) run MVO across hundreds of bootstrapped return samples and average the weights; (3) constrained optimization imposes sensible floors and caps (no more than 20% in one asset, long-only, etc.) that absorb the noise.

Practical version: most institutional allocators still use MVO as a sanity check but anchor portfolios on policy weights, peer benchmarks, or risk-factor budgets instead.

Deliverable
3-minute answer on "what is the value factor and does it still work?"
Sample answer

Definition: The value factor (HML — High Minus Low book-to-market) captures the historical excess return of cheap stocks (high B/P) over expensive stocks (low B/P). Fama-French documented it in 1992; over long horizons, value beat growth by 3–5% annualized.

The problem: Value has had a brutal decade. From 2017 through early 2020, growth crushed value by roughly 6% annualized. 2020 was catastrophic for value funds. Many declared the factor dead.

AQR's argument (Asness): The factor isn't broken — the spread just got historically stretched. Cheap stocks got cheaper, expensive stocks got more expensive. When AQR measured the "value spread" (valuation gap between cheap and expensive) in 2020, it was wider than in 1999 at the dot-com peak. That setup is exactly when forward returns to value are historically best. And indeed, 2021–2023 saw a partial value rebound.

My view: Value isn't dead but it's not a passive buy-and-hold anymore. Pure B/P is too crude — modern "quality-value" (cheap stocks that are also profitable, à la FF5 profitability + investment factors) has worked better. And the factor's behavior is regime-dependent: it works during rising-rate, rotation periods; it suffers when mega-cap growth compounds. In a 4–5% rate world with stretched Mag 7 valuations, I'd tilt toward quality-value selectively — but I wouldn't bet the farm on a pure value revival.

Deliverable
Hand-calculate Brinson attribution on a hypothetical 3-sector fund vs. benchmark.
Sample answer — worked example

Setup: Hypothetical 3-sector fund vs. benchmark. Fund returns -2.0% in the period; benchmark returns -2.5%. So fund beat by +50 bps. Attribute it.

Data:

  • Tech: Benchmark weight 30%, Fund weight 45%, Benchmark return +5%, Fund return +6%
  • Financials: Benchmark weight 40%, Fund weight 35%, Benchmark return -5%, Fund return -4%
  • Energy: Benchmark weight 30%, Fund weight 20%, Benchmark return -10%, Fund return -8%

Brinson formulas:

  • Allocation = Σ (wfund − wbench) × (rbench,sector − rbench,total)
  • Selection = Σ wbench × (rfund,sector − rbench,sector)
  • Interaction = Σ (wfund − wbench) × (rfund,sector − rbench,sector)

Allocation effect:

  • Tech: (0.45 − 0.30) × (5% − (−2.5%)) = 0.15 × 7.5% = +1.125% (overweight the winning sector)
  • Financials: (0.35 − 0.40) × (−5% − (−2.5%)) = −0.05 × −2.5% = +0.125%
  • Energy: (0.20 − 0.30) × (−10% − (−2.5%)) = −0.10 × −7.5% = +0.75%
  • Total allocation: +2.00%

Selection effect:

  • Tech: 0.30 × (6% − 5%) = +0.30%
  • Financials: 0.40 × (−4% − (−5%)) = +0.40%
  • Energy: 0.30 × (−8% − (−10%)) = +0.60%
  • Total selection: +1.30%

Interaction (residual): ~−2.80% (plug that reconciles to the +50 bps total active return). In practice this is small and often combined into Selection.

Takeaway: The fund won by both betting on Tech (allocation) and picking better names within each sector (selection). A good PM should be able to tell you which effect is dominant — persistent selection wins suggest skill; one-off allocation wins can be luck.

Deliverable
200 words on "why did risk parity break in 2022, and is it broken going forward?"
Sample answer — ~200 words

Risk parity's pitch is elegant: instead of letting equity dominate risk (as it does in a dollar-weighted 60/40 where stocks contribute ~90% of portfolio variance), allocate capital so each asset class contributes equal risk. Typically this means leveraging bonds up to match equity vol.

That worked spectacularly from the 1990s through 2020. Falling rates meant leveraged bonds produced equity-like returns with bond-like drawdowns, and the negative stock-bond correlation made the combination diversifying.

2022 broke the premise. Stocks and bonds both fell sharply as inflation and rate-hiking correlation-ed them to the downside. Leveraged bond exposure amplified the bond loss. AQR's Risk Parity fund lost ~30%; Bridgewater's All Weather was also deeply negative.

Is it broken going forward? Conditionally no. Risk parity's vulnerability was specifically the shift from a low-inflation, accommodative-Fed regime (negative stock-bond correlation) to a high-inflation, hawkish regime (positive correlation). The strategy still works in its designed environment — uncertain growth, stable inflation. But practitioners now add inflation-sensitive sleeves (commodities, TIPS, gold) and don't lever as aggressively. It's not dead, just less of a free lunch than the 2010s marketing suggested.

Deliverable
Defend concentrated vs. diversified equity book in writing. Take a position.
Sample answer — taking a position

My position: Concentrated for active mandates; diversified for core allocations. The question is wrongly framed when people treat it as binary.

The case for concentrated (20–40 names):

  • Fundamental law of active management: IR = IC × √BR. Most shops overestimate breadth — having "opinions" on 200 stocks is different from having real edge on 20. Concentrating capital behind your 20 highest-conviction ideas maximizes IC.
  • Pattern from the best long-term records (Buffett, Greenblatt, Akre): 10–25 positions, low turnover, deep work per name.
  • In a Mag 7 world, a "diversified" 500-stock fund is effectively a concentrated bet on 7 tech names. Own the concentration intentionally.

The case for diversified (100+ names):

  • Career risk asymmetry: a concentrated fund that's wrong for 18 months gets fired, even if the thesis plays out over 5. Diversification buys you time.
  • For core equity exposure in a multi-asset book, idiosyncratic risk isn't what you're trying to buy — you want market beta cheaply. Use a broad index for that.
  • Quant/systematic shops need breadth to harvest factor premia — fundamental law cuts the other way there.

Synthesis: Allocators should run a core-satellite structure. Cheap beta in the core (Vanguard/iShares/DFA). High-conviction concentrated managers as the satellite. The worst structure is "diversified-active" — 200 stocks with 80 bps of fees. That's a closet index charging active fees and it's why SPIVA data is so bleak for active mgmt.

Deliverable
Know cold: GP vs. LP, mgmt fee vs. carry, why PE returns look smoothed vs. public.
Sample answer — alternatives fundamentals cold

GP vs. LP: The General Partner manages the fund (makes investment decisions, earns fees); Limited Partners are the investors who commit capital but have limited liability and no operational control. In a PE fund, the GP might put in 1–5% of committed capital (GP commit); LPs provide the other 95%+. GP earns management fees (annual) and carried interest (performance-based).

Management fee vs. carry: Standard "2 and 20" means 2% annual management fee on committed capital + 20% carried interest on profits above a hurdle (typically 8% preferred return). The hurdle is "soft" — once crossed, GP catches up. Carry is taxed at capital gains rates in the US, which is a major lobbying issue.

Why PE returns look smoothed:

  • Privates are marked to NAV quarterly based on GP-estimated fair value, not market prices
  • NAVs lag public market movements — a PE-owned SaaS company won't get remarked daily as Snowflake stock swings
  • This creates artificially low reported vol and low correlation to public equities, making the Sharpe ratio look fantastic
  • In reality, if you mark-to-market using public comp multiples, PE looks much closer to public equity beta + 200–300 bps illiquidity premium
  • Academic research (Gredil, Sorensen, Waller) suggests reported PE Sharpe is ~2x overstated vs. "true" economic exposure

The allocator implication: PE's historical outperformance vs. public equity has been ~300 bps net of fees on a properly measured (PME) basis. Real premium, but not the "equity-like returns with bond-like risk" marketing claim. The smoothing is useful for reducing reported portfolio vol but dangerous if it masks true risk.

Deliverable
State IR = IC√BR. Explain each term and how a quant fund maximizes each.
Sample answer

The Fundamental Law of Active Management (Grinold & Kahn):

IR = IC × √BR

The terms:

  • IR (Information Ratio) = active return / active risk (tracking error). The output — your risk-adjusted alpha.
  • IC (Information Coefficient) = correlation between your forecasts and realized returns. A measure of skill. Realistic IC for a good fundamental manager is 0.02–0.05; for a top-tier quant shop, maybe 0.05–0.10. An IC of 0.5 would mean you're essentially psychic.
  • BR (Breadth) = number of independent forecasts per year. A PM making 20 stock picks per year held for a year has BR = 20. A quant running daily signals across 3,000 stocks has BR > 750,000.

How quant funds maximize each:

  • Maximize IC through signal research. Better features, cleaner data, new alternative data sources (satellite imagery, credit card transactions, NLP on earnings calls). Marginal IC improvement is hard because alpha decays as more people find it.
  • Maximize BR through scale and frequency. Cross-sectional breadth (trade 5,000 stocks instead of 50), time-series breadth (rebalance daily instead of quarterly), asset-class breadth (add futures, FX, rates to equity book). This is quant's structural edge over fundamental — a fundamental PM can't double their breadth just by adding computing power, but a quant can.

Why this matters strategically: Fundamental shops with BR ~30 need IC ~0.10 to hit an IR of 0.5. Quant shops with BR ~10,000 need IC of just 0.005. That's why high-BR/low-IC quant strategies (AQR Style Premia, DFA) can work where equivalent low-BR fundamental strategies can't.

The trap: Breadth only counts if forecasts are independent. Owning 500 stocks driven by one macro view is BR = 1, not 500.

Notes
Explain the denominator effect and why secondaries volume is rising.
Sample answer

The denominator effect: In a policy portfolio with target allocations (say 30% to private equity), privates are marked at NAV quarterly while publics are marked daily. When public markets drop 20%, the total portfolio shrinks, but privates stay near their last NAV. Suddenly privates are 35–38% of the portfolio instead of 30%. The denominator (total AUM) fell; the numerator (private NAV) didn't.

This puts allocators in a bind. They're overweight their target by IPS. They need to rebalance — either (a) sell privates (illiquid, expensive), (b) buy more publics (procyclical), or (c) ask the board to widen the policy bands. Many large endowments hit this in 2022 and again in early 2025.

Secondaries: The natural release valve. The secondaries market lets LPs sell their private fund stakes to buyers who want private exposure without committing to a new fund. Volume has gone from ~$25B in 2012 to Evercore's projected $180B+ in 2025. Two flavors:

  • LP-led secondaries: An LP sells its stake in a fund. Trades typically happen at 5–20% discounts to reported NAV, which tells you how much "smoothing" actually exists in private marks.
  • GP-led continuation vehicles: The GP sets up a new fund to acquire specific portfolio assets from the old fund, letting LPs either cash out or roll. Growing rapidly because it solves the "trapped asset" problem when the fund nears its 10-year life but the PE-owned companies can't IPO or be sold cleanly.

What this means: The secondaries boom signals that illiquidity is being priced, not just talked about. For an allocator, secondaries are now a tactical tool — buy at discounts during stress, sell during good NAV periods. For a PM building a PC/PE book, include a secondary-market execution capability.

Factors persist despite being public knowledge for three interlocking reasons:

1. Risk compensation (the efficient-markets-consistent story): Factor premia are compensation for bearing genuine risk that the market can't diversify away. Value stocks are cheap because they're often fundamentally distressed, cyclically exposed, or structurally challenged. Momentum works because trend-followers get carried out when trends reverse (2009, 2022). The factor premium isn't a free lunch — it's payment for accepting a specific risk profile. As long as the risk exists, the premium persists.

2. Behavioral/structural limits to arbitrage: Even if factors were pure mispricing, arbitrage is constrained. Institutional constraints (no shorting in mutual funds), career risk (a value manager 3 years underwater gets fired), transaction costs (small-cap + high turnover eats alpha), and capacity limits (you can't deploy $100B into a factor without moving prices) all mean rational arbitrageurs can't fully close the gap. This is why the value drawdown of 2017–2020 happened — arbitrageurs got carried out first.

3. Time horizons matter more than knowledge: Factors work on 3–7 year horizons. Most investors operate on 1–3 year horizons. That mismatch is structural — pensions rebalance annually, fund managers are evaluated quarterly, retail flows chase recent returns. Even when everyone "knows" value will mean-revert eventually, few have the horizon and discipline to harvest it through a multi-year underperformance.

The honest caveat: Factor premia do shrink over time as they become known. AQR's own research shows the value premium post-1992 (when Fama-French published) is smaller than pre-1992. Academic discovery acts as a partial tax on the premium. But "smaller" is not "zero." And new factors (quality, low-vol, momentum combinations) continue to emerge from research.

The trap to avoid: "Factor timing" — trying to predict which factor works next — is where most investors fail. Asness's "Siren Song of Factor Timing" makes the case that you're better off with stable factor exposure than trying to rotate.

When to write: on stocks you're neutral-to-slightly-bullish on, where you'd be happy selling at the strike anyway. Generates income; caps upside. Common on mature dividend payers.

Takeaway questions
(1) Difference between CPI and PCE? (2) When would the Fed cut vs. hike? (3) What's reinvestment risk and when does it matter most?
Sample answers

(1) CPI vs. PCE: Both measure consumer inflation but differ in scope and weighting. CPI (BLS) is a fixed-basket measure based on household surveys of what consumers spend. PCE (BEA) is a broader measure including what businesses and government buy on behalf of consumers (like employer-paid healthcare), and it uses a chain-weighted formula that adjusts as consumers substitute between goods. (2) Fed cut vs. hike: Cut when: growth slowing, unemployment rising, inflation below 2% target, financial stress. Fed also weighs financial stability (QE/QT balance sheet decisions are separate from rate decisions). In practice, Fed follows a Taylor-rule-like framework but with significant discretion.

(3) Reinvestment risk: The risk that cash flows from an investment must be reinvested at a lower rate than the original. Matters most for: (a) callable bonds (issuer calls when rates fall, forcing you to reinvest at lower yield); (b) mortgage-backed securities (prepayments accelerate when rates fall); (c) any high-coupon bond in a falling-rate environment. Zero-coupon bonds have no reinvestment risk during their life (but have interest rate risk). Longer-duration bonds have less reinvestment risk per dollar than short-dated bonds you keep rolling.

Sample answers

(1) Bond coupon 5%, YTM 6%: Trading at a discount. When YTM > coupon rate, the bond must be priced below par (<$1,000) so that the total return (coupon + price appreciation to par at maturity) equals the yield. Rule: YTM > coupon → discount; YTM < coupon → premium; YTM = coupon → par.

Fund creates new shares for buyers and redeems from sellers. Closed-end fund issues a fixed number of shares in an IPO; after that, shares trade on an exchange throughout the day at market-determined prices that can be at a premium or discount to NAV. Closed-end funds often trade at 5–15% discounts to NAV, which can be both an opportunity (buying $1 of assets for $0.90) and a warning sign (discount reflects manager quality or leverage risk).

Takeaway questions
(1) Sharpe ratio formula and intuition? (2) Wash sale rule — the 30-day window explained? (3) Roth IRA contribution limit 2026 and phase-out? (4) When does strategic AA trump tactical AA?
Sample answers

(1) Sharpe ratio: Sharpe = (Rp − Rf) / σp. Measures excess return per unit of total risk (standard deviation). Intuition: how much extra return you're earning above the risk-free rate, normalized by how much volatility you're taking to get it. A Sharpe of 1.0 is considered good, 2.0+ is excellent, below 0.5 is weak. Limitations: assumes normal distribution (ignores fat tails), doesn't distinguish upside vs. downside vol (Sortino fixes that), can be gamed by managers who underreport vol.

(2) Wash sale rule: The IRS disallows a capital loss deduction if you buy a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale that generated the loss (60-day total window). The disallowed loss gets added to the cost basis of the replacement security. Purpose: prevents investors from harvesting losses for tax purposes while maintaining the same economic exposure. Applies to spouses and IRAs too — you can't sell AAPL in your taxable account and buy AAPL in your IRA to dodge wash sale.

(3) Roth IRA 2026 limits: Contribution limit $7,000 ($8,000 for 50+). Income phase-out for single filers: $150K–$165K (phased out completely above $165K). Married filing jointly phase-out: $236K–$246K. Backdoor Roth (non-deductible traditional IRA → Roth conversion) is the workaround for high earners.

(4) Strategic vs. tactical: Strategic asset allocation (long-term policy weights, rebalanced to target) trumps tactical (short-term deviations based on market views) in most regimes. Academic research shows strategic allocation explains 80–90% of portfolio returns; tactical rarely adds value net of transaction costs and mistiming. Tactical makes sense only when you have genuine short-term edge (unusual market dislocation, specific catalyst), and even then, small deviations (2–5% from target) beat big ones.

Takeaway questions
(1) Calculate current yield for 5% coupon bond at $950. (2) Breakpoint sale violation example? (3) ETF vs. closed-end fund — which trades at discount/premium to NAV?
Sample answers

(1) Current yield for 5% coupon bond at $950: Current yield = Annual coupon / Current price = $50 / $950 = 5.26%. Note: current yield is different from YTM. YTM here would be higher (~5.8%) because it includes the capital gain from $950 → $1,000 (par) over the bond's remaining life. Current yield is a simpler, less complete measure.

(2) Breakpoint sale violation: Mutual funds offer reduced sales charges at purchase "breakpoints" (e.g., lower load at $25K, $50K, $100K+ investments). A breakpoint sale violation occurs when an RR splits a customer's investment into amounts just below a breakpoint to avoid the discount. Example: Customer wants to invest $52K. RR recommends $25K now, $27K next month — both transactions at full load, vs. a single $52K purchase that would qualify for the $50K breakpoint discount. This costs the customer money and pads RR commissions. It's a FINRA violation regardless of intent.

(3) ETF vs. closed-end fund NAV pricing: ETFs trade very close to NAV due to the creation/redemption mechanism — authorized participants arbitrage any price/NAV gap by creating or redeeming shares directly with the fund in exchange for underlying securities. Typical deviation from NAV: less than 20 bps. Closed-end funds have no such arbitrage mechanism (fixed share count), so market price can diverge significantly from NAV based on supply/demand for the shares. CEFs frequently trade at 5–15% discounts (reflecting leverage concerns, manager uncertainty, or structural illiquidity) or premiums (reflecting strong demand, scarce strategy).

Takeaway questions
(1) Bull call spread max gain/loss/breakeven? (2) Tax-equivalent yield for 4% muni at 32% bracket? (3) When would you write a covered call vs. buy a protective put?
Sample answers

(1) Bull call spread — Example: Buy 50 call for $5, sell 55 call for $2.

  • Net debit (cost): $5 − $2 = $3
  • Max loss: $3 (if stock stays at or below $50 at expiration)
  • Max gain: ($55 − $50) − $3 = $2 (if stock is at or above $55 at expiration)
  • Breakeven: $50 + $3 = $53 (long strike + net debit)
  • Use when: moderately bullish, want to reduce cost of call by capping upside at a known level

(2) Tax-equivalent yield (4% muni, 32% bracket): TEY = Muni yield / (1 − tax rate) = 4% / (1 − 0.32) = 4% / 0.68 = 5.88%. Meaning: a taxable bond would need to yield 5.88% to give you the same after-tax return as the 4% muni. If a comparable-risk corporate bond yields 5%, the muni wins. If it yields 6%, the corporate wins after-tax for this investor. Note: this is federal-only; if the muni is also state-tax-exempt for the investor, TEY is even higher.

(3) Covered call vs. protective put:

  • Covered call: Own stock + sell (short) call. Generates income, caps upside. Use when: neutral-to-slightly-bullish, happy to sell at strike, want to earn premium. Outlook: "I don't expect the stock to go much higher soon."
  • Protective put: Own stock + buy (long) put. Costs premium, provides downside insurance. Use when: bullish long-term but worried about near-term downside, willing to pay for insurance. Outlook: "I love this stock but I'm nervous about earnings/Fed/macro event next month."
  • Key difference: covered call trades away upside for income; protective put pays premium for downside protection. Opposite ends of the P&L spectrum.

Example: Stock purchased for $20K with $10K equity (50% initial). Stock falls to $14K. Current equity = $14K − $10K (loan) = $4K. Required equity at 25% = $14K × 0.25 = $3,500. Takeaway questions
(1) Why does duration matter more than YTM when rates shift? (2) Sharpe vs. Sortino vs. Treynor — when to use each? (3) Standard V(A) vs. V(B) of CFA Code — key difference?
Sample answers

(1) Why duration matters more than YTM when rates shift: YTM is a point-in-time yield measure — what you earn if you hold to maturity at current prices. But when rates change, bond prices move, and the magnitude of that move depends on duration, not YTM. A 10-yr bond with 5% YTM and modified duration of 8 loses ~8% of value on a 100 bps rate rise; a 2-yr bond with the same 5% YTM and duration 1.9 loses only ~1.9%. YTM tells you the expected return if nothing changes; duration tells you the sensitivity to change. For active management, duration is the actionable number — it's what you hedge, budget, and attribute P&L against.

(2) Sharpe vs. Sortino vs. Treynor:

  • Sharpe = (Rp − Rf) / σp. Risk is total volatility (standard deviation). Use for: comparing investments as standalone portfolios where all vol matters. Best for: hedge funds, diversified portfolios in isolation.
  • Sortino = (Rp − Rf) / downside deviation. Only penalizes returns below a target (usually zero or risk-free). Use for: strategies with asymmetric return distributions (convexity plays, trend-following) where upside vol is good. Best for: evaluating "positively skewed" strategies.
  • Treynor = (Rp − Rf) / βp. Risk is systematic (market) beta, not total vol. Use for: comparing assets or sub-portfolios within a well-diversified book, where idiosyncratic risk washes out and only beta matters. Best for: a PM evaluating contribution of a sleeve to a larger diversified portfolio.

Rule of thumb: Sharpe for standalone evaluation; Sortino when you believe upside shouldn't count against you; Treynor when the investment is one piece of a larger diversified whole.

(3) Standard V(A) vs. V(B): These are from the CFA Code of Ethics — Standard V is "Investment Analysis, Recommendations, and Actions."

  • V(A) Diligence and Reasonable Basis: Members must exercise diligence, independence, and thoroughness in analyses; have a reasonable basis supported by appropriate research and investigation for any recommendation. Essentially: "do the work before recommending."
  • V(B) Communication with Clients and Prospective Clients: Members must disclose to clients the basic format and general principles of the investment process, promptly disclose any material changes, distinguish fact from opinion, and communicate relevant factors. Essentially: "tell clients honestly how you think and what's going on."

Key difference: V(A) is about the analytical work behind the recommendation; V(B) is about how you communicate it to clients. You could do rigorous V(A) analysis but violate V(B) by hiding assumptions, glossing over risks, or presenting opinion as fact. Both are required; they're distinct obligations.
